Three Approaches to Value

Cost Approach
Estimate cost to reproduce/replace improvements, subtract depreciation, add land value. Best for new or unique properties.
Comparative Sales
Compare subject to recently sold similar properties with adjustments. Preferred approach when reliable data is available.
Income Approach
Convert income stream into present value via capitalization. Best for income-producing investment properties.

CH 1 Theory of Value

What is an Appraisal?

Appraisal is the process of estimating the value of specific property at a stated time and place. A formal appraisal is supportable based on facts, data, and methods, and is backed by a written report. Three fundamental problems: (1) define value, (2) define the property rights to be valued, (3) determine the proper method.

Prerequisites of Economic Value

Before any good can have value, it must meet all four prerequisites:

  • Utility — capacity to evoke desire; "wantedness"
  • Scarcity — under-supply relative to demand
  • Capacity for Private Ownership — can be exclusively owned
  • Demand (Effective Purchasing Power) — desire backed by ability to pay

Market Value vs. Use Value

Market Value (Value in Exchange) — the price a property brings in the open market; determined by supply and demand. Use Value — the value a specific property has for a specific use, without regard to highest and best use.

Price, Cost, and Value

  • Price — the amount actually paid in a specific transaction
  • Cost — the expenditure required to produce/develop property (direct + indirect costs)
  • Value — the present worth of future benefits; may differ from price or cost
  • Direct Costs — expenditures for labor and materials ("hard costs")
  • Indirect Costs — items other than labor/materials: legal fees, financing, admin ("soft costs")

Other Types of Value

  • Assessed Value — taxable value upon which tax rate is applied
  • Insurable Value — value for insurance purposes
  • Going-Concern Value — total value of an operating business enterprise
  • Book Value — asset value recorded on a balance sheet
  • Investment Value — value to a specific investor based on individual requirements

CH 2 Standard of Value for Property Tax Appraisal

Fair Market Value (Revenue and Taxation Code §110)

The standard for California property tax. Defined as the amount of cash or equivalent value that property would bring if exposed for sale in the open market under conditions in which:

  • Both buyer and seller have full knowledge of all uses and purposes of the property
  • The property is exposed for sale for a reasonable time
  • Neither party is compelled to act (no exigencies)
  • The price is expressed in money terms

Section 110 creates a rebuttable presumption that the actual purchase price is fair market value if negotiated under open market conditions.

Appraisal Unit

The unit that people in the market buy and sell. Referred to as the "unit to be appraised." For single-family residences, it includes the combination of land and buildings.

Principle of Unit Valuation — value accrues to assets because of their ability to generate benefits as a team rather than as a sum of parts. Unitary assets are appraised together; non-unitary assets are separate appraisal units.

Limited Market Properties

Properties with relatively few potential buyers: large manufacturing plants, railroad sidings, R&D properties, unique design features. If likely to remain in current use → value based on present use. If use likely to change → value based on alternative use.

Restricted Value Exceptions

Certain properties are assessed at restricted value (based on restricted use, not highest and best use):

  • Open space under Williamson Act contracts
  • Historical properties with preservation contracts
  • Golf courses (≥10 acres, ≥24 months in use)
  • Timberland Production Zones
  • Certain single-family residences

CH 3 Property Concepts

Types of Property

  • Real Property — possession, claim to, ownership of, or right to possession of land; mines, minerals, quarries; standing timber; improvements
  • Personal Property — all property except real property (tangible and intangible)
  • Tangible Personal Property — can be seen, weighed, measured, felt, or touched (excluding land and improvements)
  • Intangible Personal Property — notes, shares of stock, bonds, deeds of trust, mortgages, money — generally exempt from taxation

What is Taxable?

  • All property is taxable unless exempt by the California Constitution or statute
  • Both real and personal property can be taxable
  • All taxable property is assessed at the same percentage of fair market value
  • Intangible assets are generally exempt; their value shall not enhance taxable property value

Land Classifications

For property tax purposes, "land" includes: natural vegetation, quarries, wells, mines, felled timber (and the land itself).

Fixtures

Originally personal property, classified as real property when physically or constructively annexed to real property with the intent that it remain indefinitely. Tests: physical annexation, constructive annexation, owner's intent. Examples: huge printing presses, vault doors, permanently bolted walkways.

Water Rights

California recognizes three types: Riparian (tied to land adjacent to water), Appropriative (prior appropriation doctrine), and Pueblo rights.

Transfers of Development Rights (TDR)

A development right separated from a landowner's bundle of rights and transferred (generally by sale) to another landowner in the same or different area.

Spatial Levels of Real Estate

  • Surface — rights to use the earth and everything attached to it
  • Above-Surface — air rights, extends upward from surface
  • Sub-surface — mineral and petroleum rights, down to center of earth

Legal (Ownership) Interests

  • Fee Simple Estate — most complete form of ownership; absolute, unencumbered, subject only to: Taxation, Eminent Domain, Police Power, Escheat
  • Life Estate — interest held for the grantee's lifetime; passes to the remainderman at death
  • Leasehold — lessee's right to use property during lease term; lessor = grantor/landlord; lessee = tenant
  • Easements — right of use over another's property
  • Liens — creditor's right to secure debt payment from property
  • CC&Rs — contractual restrictions on property use within a subdivision

Forms of Concurrent Ownership

  • Tenancy in Common — no right of survivorship; fractional interests
  • Joint Tenancy — right of survivorship
  • Community Property — between spouses
  • Time-Share — ownership divided by specific time periods

CH 4 Economic Framework for Real Property Appraisal

The Real Estate Market

A market is the organized action between buyers (demand) and sellers (supply) that permits trade. Real estate markets are classified by geographic boundaries, property type, or scope of participants.

Special Characteristics of Real Estate

  • Immobility (fixed location)
  • Uniqueness (heterogeneity)
  • Durability
  • High unit value
  • Local market nature

Forces Affecting Real Property Value

  • Physical Forces — topography, soil, climate, infrastructure, scenic beauty, parcel size and shape
  • Demographic Forces — population trends, household characteristics, lifestyles
  • Economic Forces — employment, income, financing availability, interest rates, rental/price patterns
  • Governmental Forces — zoning, building codes, environmental regulations, fire/police/health protection

Real Estate Productivity

In real estate, productivity is used as a synonym for utility. Physical aspect concerns natural characteristics and man-made improvements.

Off-site improvements (infrastructure): sewage, water, drainage, electric/gas/telephone.
On-site improvements: landscaping, retaining walls, walkways, driveways, utility connections.

Highest and Best Use

The use that is: Legally permissible, Physically possible, Financially feasible, and Maximally productive.

Two distinct concepts: (1) Highest and best use as though vacant — what should be built?; (2) Highest and best use as improved — should the existing use be continued, intensified, or changed?

Interim Use — temporary use before transition to the highest and best use; appears in transitional areas.
Consistent Use — a property must be valued on the basis of a single use; can't value land for one use and improvements for another.

Excess vs. Surplus Land

  • Excess Land — more land than needed; can be sold separately; should be appraised separately; has significant utility and value
  • Surplus Land — does not support existing improvement; cannot be sold separately; cannot be separated from the appraisal unit

Fundamental Appraisal Principles

Productivity Principles:

  • Anticipation — value is the present worth of anticipated future benefits (NOT based on historical performance)
  • Contribution — value of a component equals its contribution to the whole
  • Agents of Production — Land (ground + airspace + natural resources), Labor (all wages and operating expenses involving human work), Capital, Entrepreneurship (visualizing need, taking action and risk to produce)
  • Surplus Productivity — net income remaining after costs of labor, entrepreneurship, and capital are satisfied

Marketability Principles:

  • Supply and Demand — excess supply → price falls; excess demand → price rises
  • Substitution — upper limit of value set by cost of acquiring an equally desirable substitute
  • Externalities — external activities can have positive or negative effects on property value
  • Change — recognizes the dynamic nature of real estate markets
  • Conformity — maximum value when reasonable degree of homogeneity exists in the neighborhood
  • Regression and Progression — value of superior property is adversely affected by inferior neighboring properties (regression); value of inferior property is enhanced by superior neighbors (progression)

CH 5 Measurement of Value

Steps in the Appraisal Process

  1. Definition of the appraisal problem
  2. Preliminary analysis and data collection
  3. Highest and best use analysis
  4. Land value estimate
  5. Application of the approaches to value
  6. Reconciliation of value indicators and the final value estimate
  7. Reporting the final value estimate

Land Valuation

Land is identified and described, then analyzed for site characteristics. Methods of land appraisal include comparative sales, allocation, extraction, land residual, and ground rent capitalization.

Linkages

Recurring interactions between different land uses at different geographic points are referred to as linkages.

CH 6 Approaches to Value

Cost Approach

Three-step process:

  1. Estimate the current cost to reproduce or replace the existing structure
  2. Deduct for all accrued depreciation
  3. Add the estimated land value (+ entrepreneurial profit if present)

Reproduction Cost — cost to build an exact replica using same materials/methods.
Replacement Cost — cost to build a property with equivalent utility using modern materials/methods.
Historical Cost — total cost when originally constructed.

Cost Estimation Methods:

  • Quantity Survey Method — most detailed; estimates all material and labor costs
  • Square Foot (Comparative Unit) Method — uses known costs of similar buildings per square foot
  • Unit-In-Place Method — adds unit costs of each component (foundation, walls, roof)
  • Trended Historical Cost — adjusts original cost forward using trending tables

Three Types of Depreciation:

  • Physical Deterioration — loss from physical wear and tear; can be curable or incurable
  • Functional Obsolescence — loss from changes in desirability/design; curable or incurable
  • External (Economic) Obsolescence — loss from external negative influences outside the property; always incurable

Percent Good = 100% − Depreciation%. If 20% depreciated, percent good = 80%.

Comparative Sales Approach

Compare the subject property to recently sold comparable properties. Under Rule 4, this is the preferred approach when reliable market data are available. Adjustments are made for differences using elements of comparison (location, size, age, condition, etc.).

Units of Comparison: price per sq ft, price per unit, price per room, price per leasable sq ft.

Income Approach

Converts income into value through capitalization. Key concepts:

  • Potential Gross Income (PGI) — total income before deducting vacancy or expenses
  • Effective Gross Income (EGI) — PGI minus vacancy and collection losses
  • Net Income Before Recapture (NIBR&T) — EGI minus operating expenses
  • Overall Rate (Ro) — ratio of NIBR to sale price; implied return
  • Gross Income Multiplier (GIM) — Sale price ÷ Gross Income
  • Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM) — Sale price ÷ Gross Rent (principal improvements only)

Capitalization Methods:

  • Direct Capitalization — converts single year's income by dividing by a rate or multiplying by a factor
  • Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) — applies discount rate to a series of projected income payments
  • Yield Capitalization — considers income pattern, value change, and yield rate

Contract Rent = actual rent per lease. Market (Economic) Rent = what the property would rent for on the open market.

Reconciliation

After applying approaches, the appraiser reconciles the value indicators into a final estimate. The approach(es) given most weight depend on the type of property, data reliability, and purpose of the appraisal.

CH 7 Personal Property Appraisal Program

Personal property differs from real property in that it is generally movable and not permanently annexed to land.

Key Concepts

  • Situs — place where personal property is located for tax purposes
  • Trade Level — the level of the distribution chain at which property is assessed
  • Lien Date — January 1, 12:01 a.m. — date as of which all property is assessed annually

Generally Exempt Personal Property

  • Household furnishings and personal effects
  • Business inventories
  • Intangible assets (notes, bonds, stocks, mortgages, money held for business use)

Appraisal Methods

  • Audit-Appraisal Method — reviews taxpayer's books and records
  • Physical Appraisal Method — physical inspection and valuation of assets

CH 8 / APP Special Topics & Administration

Manufactured Homes (Mobilehomes)

Classification as real or personal property depends on permanency of installation. Site value is assessed separately.

Construction in Progress

Start date and completion date determine how new construction is assessed on the supplemental roll.

Proposition 13 (1978)

  • Limited property tax rate to 1% plus voter-approved bond rates
  • Assessment based on base year value (acquisition value or 1975 value)
  • Annual increase limited to 2% or the Consumer Price Index, whichever is less
  • Reassessment triggered by change in ownership or new construction

Assessment Process Key Dates

  • Lien Date: January 1 — property assessed as of this date
  • Assessment Roll: lists all taxable property, owner name/address, assessed value, exemptions
  • Escape Assessments: for property that was not assessed when it should have been

Decline in Value (Prop 8)

If market value falls below the base year value, the assessor must use the lower market value for that tax year.
